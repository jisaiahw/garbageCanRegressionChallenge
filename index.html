Analysis of Time vs Anxiety Relationship

Visual Assessment of the Time-Anxiety Scatter Plot:

  1. Linear Relationship: The scatter plot shows a positive linear relationship between Time and Anxiety, but it’s weaker than the StressSurvey relationship
  2. Data Clustering: Notice that the data points cluster around specific Time values (0, 1, 2, 2.1, 2.2), creating distinct vertical bands
  3. Regression Line Fit: The red regression line shows a positive slope, but the confidence intervals are wider, indicating more uncertainty
  4. Residuals Pattern: The residuals vs fitted plot helps identify any systematic patterns in the model errors

Key Issues with the Time Model:

  1. Limited Time Variation:
    • Time values are clustered around only 5 distinct points (0, 1, 2, 2.1, 2.2)
    • This limited variation makes it difficult to establish a strong linear relationship
    • The small range of Time values (0 to 2.2) constrains the model’s ability to capture the true relationship
  2. Omitted Variable Bias:
    • The true relationship is Anxiety = Stress + 0.1 × Time
    • By omitting Stress, the Time coefficient absorbs the effect of the omitted Stress variable
    • Since Time and Stress are correlated, the estimated Time coefficient will be biased upward from the true value of 0.1
  3. Model Specification Issues:
    • The bivariate model assumes that Time alone explains Anxiety, ignoring the primary effect of Stress
    • This leads to a misspecified model that doesn’t reflect the true data-generating process
  4. Lower Model Fit:
    • The R-squared for the Time model will be lower than the StressSurvey model
    • This reflects the fact that Time alone explains less variance in Anxiety compared to StressSurvey

Data Pattern Insights: - The grouped data shows that higher Time values tend to have higher Anxiety, but this is largely driven by the correlation with Stress - The relationship appears linear within the observed range, but the limited Time variation makes this relationship less reliable

Interpretation of the Scatter Plot and Model Fit

Visual Assessment: 1. StressSurvey vs Anxiety: Shows a strong positive linear relationship with good fit 2. Time vs Anxiety: Shows a weaker but still positive linear relationship 3. Regression Lines: Both models show reasonable fit to the data with confidence intervals

Model Fit Statistics Comparison: - Model 1 (StressSurvey): Higher R-squared, indicating better fit - Model 2 (Time): Lower R-squared, indicating weaker relationship - Both models are statistically significant (low p-values)

Coefficient Analysis:

Model 1 (Anxiety ~ StressSurvey): - The coefficient on StressSurvey will be biased upward from the true Stress coefficient of 1.0 - This happens because StressSurvey is highly correlated with Stress, and the omitted Time variable’s effect gets absorbed into the StressSurvey coefficient - The estimated coefficient captures both the direct effect of Stress and the indirect effect through correlation with Time

Model 2 (Anxiety ~ Time): - The coefficient on Time will be biased upward from the true Time coefficient of 0.1 - This happens because Time is correlated with Stress, and the omitted Stress variable’s effect gets absorbed into the Time coefficient - The estimated coefficient captures both the direct effect of Time (0.1) and the indirect effect through correlation with Stress

Key Insights:

  1. Omitted Variable Bias in Both Models: Both bivariate models suffer from omitted variable bias because they exclude important variables from the true relationship

  2. Correlation Effects:

    • StressSurvey and Stress are highly correlated, so Model 1 captures most of the true relationship
    • Time and Stress are moderately correlated, so Model 2 captures some of the Stress effect in the Time coefficient

  3. Model Performance:

    • Model 1 (StressSurvey) will have higher R-squared because StressSurvey is a better proxy for the true Stress variable
    • Model 2 (Time) will have lower R-squared because Time alone explains less of the variance in Anxiety

  4. True vs Estimated Relationships:

    • True: Anxiety = Stress + 0.1 × Time
    • Model 1: Anxiety = β₀ + β₁ × StressSurvey (where β₁ > 1.0 due to bias)
    • Model 2: Anxiety = γ₀ + γ₁ × Time (where γ₁ > 0.1 due to bias)

This demonstrates the classic “garbage can regression” problem where omitting important variables leads to biased coefficient estimates, even when individual models appear to fit the data reasonably well.
